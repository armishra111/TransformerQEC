# TransformerQEC

**A Physics-Informed Transformer Decoder for Quantum Error Correction on Rotated Surface Codes**

We train a Transformer encoder to perform classification on syndrome data generated by STIM under a **phenomenological noise model** for the **rotated surface code**. Given a syndrome bit-string $\mathbf{s} \in \{0, 1\}^{N_d}$ from $N_d$ detectors and a physical error rate $p$, the model predicts whether a logical $\hat{Z}_L$ error has occurred:

$$\hat{y} = f_\theta(\mathbf{s}, p) \in \{0, 1\}$$

The checked-in artifacts evaluate the decoder against the **Minimum Weight Perfect Matching (MWPM)** baseline (via PyMatching) across code distances $d \in \{3, 5, 7\}$. The current results record strong $d=3$ performance, mixed $d=5$ performance, and underperformance at $d=7$ relative to MWPM.

## 1. Architecture

**Model Overview:** The checked-in baseline config uses a JAX/Flax Transformer encoder with `d_model=128`, `num_heads=4`, `head_dim=32`, distance-specific layer counts `{3: 4, 5: 6, 7: 6}`, and default `float32` compute. The physical error rate $p$ is continuously injected via an MLP into the token embeddings to support evaluation across noise regimes.

**Physics-Informed (2+1)D RoPE:** Standard 1D positional encodings discard the $(x, y, t)$ lattice structure. This repo uses a **(2+1)D Anisotropic RoPE** to decompose detector coordinates into orthogonal spatial and temporal subspaces.

* **Non-Lorentzian Frame:** Under phenomenological noise, qubit decoherence (spatial correlation) and measurement errors (temporal correlation) operate on fundamentally distinct coordinate axes. 

* **Capacity Allocation:** We partition the RoPE frequency bands into a 3:1 spatial-to-temporal ratio, reflecting the richer structure of the 2D stabilizer lattice compared to the 1D temporal chain.

The intended inductive bias is an **anisotropic attention kernel** that can represent spatial and temporal correlations additively while preserving the detector-coordinate structure:

$$A(i, j) \propto \exp\left(\frac{\mathbf{q}^{(xy)}_i \cdot \mathbf{k}^{(xy)}_j + \mathbf{q}^{(t)}_i \cdot \mathbf{k}^{(t)}_j}{\sqrt{d_{\text{head}}}}\right)$$

## 2. Training

* **Data Pipeline (STIM):** The checked-in baseline config declares `6000000 total training samples` across distances $d \in \{3,5,7\}$, generated via STIM (`surface_code:rotated_memory_z`) under phenomenological noise. Training error rates are geometrically sampled from $p \in [0.002, 0.017]$ and evaluation rates from $p \in [0.0015, 0.01]$.
* **Objective (Focal Loss):** To counteract the extreme class imbalance at low $p$ regimes (where logical errors constitute $\sim 0.02\%$ of samples), we optimize using Focal Loss ($\gamma=2.0, \alpha=0.75$):
  $$\mathcal{L}_{\text{focal}}(p_t) = -\alpha_t \,(1 - p_t)^\gamma \,\log(p_t)$$

### 3.1 Decoder Performance Comparison

The Transformer decoder is evaluated against the MWPM baseline across three code distances. MWPM decoding is performed via PyMatching using STIM's detector error model.

<p align="center"><b>Figure 1.</b> Logical error rate vs. physical error rate for the Transformer decoder (solid) and MWPM (dashed) across code distances <i>d</i> = 3, 5, 7. The checked-in CSV records consistent improvement at <i>d</i> = 3, near parity or mixed results at <i>d</i> = 5, and MWPM superiority at <i>d</i> = 7.</p>

**Key results at $p = 0.01$:**

| Distance | $N_{\text{det}}$ | MWPM $p_L$ | Transformer $p_L$ | Relative Improvement |
|:---:|:---:|:---:|:---:|:---:|
| $d = 3$ | 24 | $8.57 \times 10^{-3}$ | $6.90 \times 10^{-3}$ | **19.5% Reduction** |
| $d = 5$ | 120 | $2.31 \times 10^{-3}$ | $2.33 \times 10^{-3}$ | **Match** |
| $d = 7$ | 336 | $3.60 \times 10^{-4}$ | $1.61 \times 10^{-3}$ | *4.5x worse* |

At $d = 3$, the Transformer consistently outperforms MWPM across the evaluated range with roughly $10\text{--}29\%$ improvement. At $d = 5$, the checked-in CSV is close to parity across much of the range with small wins and losses. At $d = 7$, MWPM is better across the displayed high-end points, so mechanism and scaling explanations should be treated as hypotheses for future analysis rather than proven conclusions.

<p align="center"><b>Figure 2.</b> Log-log plot of recorded logical error rates. All three distances exhibit the expected monotonic decrease of <i>p<sub>L</sub></i> with decreasing <i>p</i>.</p>

#### Log-Log Regression

Under the surface code power-law $P_L = C \cdot (p/p_{\text{th}})^{(d+1)/2}$, a log-log regression of `transformer_ler` on `p` should yield slope $(d+1)/2$. Fitting separately per distance (zeros excluded):

| d | Fitted slope | Theory $(d+1)/2$ | Implied $p_{\text{th}}$ | $\log_{10}(C)$ | $R^2$ |
|---|:---:|:---:|:---:|:---:|:---:|
| 3 | 2.003 | 2.0 | ~3.8% | −4.2 | 0.9993 |
| 5 | 3.010 | 3.0 | ~3.2% | −7.3 | 0.9988 |
| 7 | 4.205 | 4.0 | ~2.8% | −9.9 | 0.9965 |

<sub>Data: results/evaluation_results.csv</sub>

**Takeaways:** Slopes land within ~5% of theory ($R^2 > 0.996$) in the recorded regression, which is consistent with expected surface-code power-law scaling. Implied thresholds sit in the expected ~1–4% range. The prefactor $C$ drops by five orders of magnitude from $d=3$ to $d=7$ in this fit.

## 4. Insights & Future Directions

* **Mechanism hypotheses:** Correlated defect signatures, including possible $Y$-error structure, are plausible explanations for the $d=3$ gains, but the checked-in artifacts do not isolate that mechanism. Treat this as future ablation work.
* **Generalization analysis:** The recorded log-log fits are consistent with expected surface-code scaling, but stronger out-of-distribution and homological generalization claims need dedicated held-out experiments and written comparisons.
* **The $d=7$ gap:** The checked-in artifacts show the current Transformer underperforms MWPM at $d=7$. Capacity, optimization, and data scale are candidate explanations, not established conclusions.
* **Circuit-Level Noise:** Future work will swap the phenomenological assumption for full circuit-level simulations (matching the *AlphaQubit* baseline) to test the robustness of the (2+1)D RoPE inductive bias against spatial crosstalk and leakage.
* **Inference Latency:** While $O(L^2)$ attention poses asymptotic challenges compared to optimized $O(L^3)$ MWPM C++ implementations, future benchmarks will quantify the throughput-accuracy tradeoff using JAX batched inference on TPU hardware.
* **Power-Law Scaling Check:** Log-log regression yields fitted slopes within ~5% of the theoretical $(d+1)/2$ exponents ($R^2 > 0.996$), with implied thresholds in the expected ~1–4% range and prefactor $C$ shrinking rapidly with $d$ in the recorded fit.
